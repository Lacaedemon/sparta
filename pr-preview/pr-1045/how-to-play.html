How to Play

Controls, the objective, and the moment-to-moment battle loop.
Published

Last updated: 2026-07-23

You command the blue army (top of the field). Your objective: defeat the red army (bottom). A side loses when it has no units left on the field — every one destroyed or fled clear of the map. A unit that is merely routing still counts: it’s off fighting for now, but the battle stays live while it’s still on the field, whether it’s “broken” (still able to rally) or has “shattered” (lost its nerve for good, but still has to either run for it or be run down before it’s actually gone).

Controls

A right-click on empty ground is a move order; a right-click on an enemy is an attack order. Selection and camera stay live even while paused, so you can plan under pressure.

Control bar. When you have units selected, a control bar appears at the bottom of the screen with clickable buttons for the most-used unit toggles. Use it as a mouse-driven alternative to the hotkeys whenever you prefer clicking to typing.

  • Formation — click Normal, Tight, Loose, or Square (Orbis) directly (same as T), or pick one of the other direct-select stances: Schiltron (Shift + O, the harder-bracing anti-cavalry variant), Shield Wall (L), or Testudo (U). T cycles only the density set plus Square; the other three stances are set from the menu or their direct keys.
  • Stance — click any order stance to arm it for your next right-click order (same as the stance hotkeys; Esc / the Normal button clears it).
  • Reform — toggle Reform before move for the selected unit(s) (same as the checkbox in the info panel).
  • Focused / Spread — cycle the group-order mode (same as X); governs both a multi-unit attack and a multi-unit relief.

The bar updates immediately when you select a different unit or change a setting elsewhere.

Pace modes (AUTO by default). Units walk on approach, jog when a ranged enemy is within range (under missile fire), and accelerate to full sprint speed once within about 200 px of the target — so a charge arrives at full momentum without wearing out the troops on the march. Check Walk advance in the selected-unit info panel to lock the selection to walk pace throughout; this is required for formed stances like shield wall or pike phalanx that break if the unit breaks into a run. Each unit type has its own default — spearmen start with it on, everyone else off — and the setting persists per unit until you (or a replayed order) change it again.

Move order sequence. When you right-click a destination, the regiment turns to face it and marches there in good order: it pivots about its centre onto the new heading — turning in place before it advances on a sharp turn — marches, and settles back into formation on arrival. The next two sections cover the special cases: a small sideways shift (side-step) and a larger turn (centre pivot).

Disciplined vs. undisciplined units. The centre-pivot march above is what a disciplined (trained, professional) unit executes. An undisciplined unit — a levy or mob — skips it: it turns to face its destination immediately and walks there directly, re-forming as its soldiers arrive rather than turning as one formed body. A disciplined unit given an in-haste order — a triple- or quadruple-click move, i.e. run or sprint pace — marches the same undisciplined way for that order: there’s no time to execute a formed turn at a dead run.

Reform before move (default on for most types). By default, a fresh move order makes a unit hold its position briefly (~0.8 s) while its ranks settle into formation before it steps off. The unit spends this hold pivoting in place toward the destination — no faster than its corner man can run, so the ranks hold their dressing — and its ranks are already coming onto the new heading before the first step. For a unit already on the march, the hold is also the brake leg of a sharp turn: a fresh order that roughly continues the current travel carries the column’s momentum straight through, while one that turns it sharply back bleeds that speed off during the hold, so the block comes about from a stand instead of carrying its pace into the turn (Figure 3). To make a unit move immediately without the pause, uncheck Reform before move in its info panel, or click Reform in the control bar — either applies to every currently selected unit. Cavalry starts with the setting off (immediate responsiveness matters more to a mounted regiment than settling ranks); every other type starts with it on. The setting is a persistent property of each unit, and any mid-battle toggle is itself recorded, so replays reproduce whichever mode was active at the moment you issued each order.

Side-step (small lateral moves). A move order that only shifts a unit a short way to the side — less than about its own width — is carried out as a side-step: the unit shuffles sideways at a walk while holding its current facing, instead of pivoting the whole line round to face the move and then pivoting back. So a small dressing of the line keeps every soldier looking the same way and keeps the ranks in order. A move that heads forward still turns to face the destination and marches normally; a longer move to the side is a lateral pivot instead, covered below.

Back-step (short moves to the rear). The same short-distance exception applies behind the unit: a move order that only shifts it a short way straight back is a back-step — it shuffles backward at a walk while holding its facing, the same nudge as the ↓ arrow key below. A longer move into the rear arc is a real repositioning march instead, covered next.

Arrow-key nudges. With a unit selected, the arrow keys order a small fixed step without touching the mouse: ← and → side-step the unit a few metres to that side, ↑ steps it forward, and ↓ back-steps it a few metres straight to the rear. All four hold the unit’s facing — a nudge shuffles it into place, it never turns to face the step. Use them to dress a line, open a gap, close up a short distance, or edge a regiment off a bad spot. While a unit is selected these four arrows nudge instead of panning the camera; WASD still pans, so you never lose camera control.

Disengage and step back (Ctrl + ↓). The plain back-step nudge above refuses to fire on a unit currently in melee — a drill step shouldn’t yank a regiment out of a fight by accident. Ctrl + ↓ is the deliberate, combat-legal version: it breaks contact and steps the unit back a short distance, holding facing the same way. A controlled withdrawal to reset spacing, not a retreat — but it isn’t free: the enemy’s own attack keeps landing hits (with the flank/rear bonus) for as long as it can still reach you, so the fight has to actually be worth walking away from.

Centre pivot (larger turns). A move order that turns a unit toward a new heading makes it pivot about its centre there gradually rather than snapping — it turns toward its travel direction in good order as it sets off. When the heading change is sharp, the unit pivots before it advances: it nearly halts, comes onto its new facing, then marches. So a unit always marches facing its destination, and never reverses into it. (A chasing or charging unit still turns instantly — the gradual pivot is for your own move orders, where order matters more than the last fraction of a second.)

Move to the rear (about-face, reform, then march). A longer move order to a destination roughly behind the unit (more than 135° off its facing) is not a centre pivot at all: the regiment first performs the standing about-face (conversio, below) — every soldier reverses 180° where they stand, the front rank becoming the rear rank man-for-man — and then marches to the destination facing it. The block keeps its footprint the whole time rather than swinging the whole line round about its centre, so a unit sent to the rear turns about cleanly and steps off, never sliding backwards.

Because the flip makes the old rear rank the new front, a unit that has taken losses would otherwise lead with its thinnest rank — the short, under-strength rank that had been tucked safely at the back. So after the turn the regiment reforms: the men counter-march through the block onto ranks squared to the new heading, putting a full rank back in front and returning the short rank to the rear. With Reform before move on (the default) this happens before the unit steps off; with it off, the unit marches at once — short rank leading — and reforms on arrival instead.

Move to the rear, oblique (about-face, flank pivot, then march). When that rear-sector destination is oblique enough — more than about 30° off the reversed heading — the about-face’s own 180° reversal alone would still leave a sizeable turn uncorrected. For a move that oblique, the regiment adds one more standing step before it marches: once the about-face completes, it flank-pivots (wheels) the rest of the way onto the destination’s bearing — the same hinge swing as the Z/C drill below, just chained in automatically — and only then steps off, already lined up on its destination instead of leaving the last of the turn to the march’s own gradual centre pivot.

Lateral pivot (quarter-turn, file march, quarter-turn back). A longer move order to one side — too far to side-step, but not far enough off-facing to count as behind the unit — is a file march: the regiment quarter-turns toward the destination’s side in place, marches straight there keeping its pre-turn footprint the whole way (the block never reforms to face the new heading, so a wide line stays wide instead of narrowing into a column), and then quarter-turns back to its original facing once it arrives. The unit ends up facing exactly the way it started — the reorientation is temporary, for the march only, not a permanent repointing of the whole regiment.

About-face in place (conversio, V). Press V with a unit selected to issue the conversio: every soldier reverses 180° where they stand — the file/rank grid stays put, the front rank becomes the rear rank man-for-man, and the unit is now facing the other way. Unlike the gradual centre pivot that accompanies a move order (which rotates the whole block around its centre as it marches), the conversio is a standing drill command: no one takes a step. Use it to face a threat behind the line without marching first, or to quickly reorient a reserve before it moves up.

Quarter-turn in place (Q / E). Press Q to turn the selected unit a quarter-turn left, or E to turn it right: every soldier pivots 90° where they stand. No one marches and the grid of men does not reorganize — they simply face the new way, so the block now presents its short edge as its frontage (its frontage and depth swap relative to the facing, without anyone moving). Like the conversio, it is a standing facing change. Use it to throw a fresh face toward a flank, or as the first step of marching off to the side in good order.

Wheel on a flank (circumductio, Z / C). Press Z to wheel the selected unit left, or C to wheel it right. One end of the line — the standing flank file — holds its ground while the rest of the block swings around it like a door on a hinge, reorienting the whole line 90° while every man keeps his place in the ranks. Unlike the quarter-turn, this is not a standing facing change: the far flank actually marches an arc, so the line ends up facing a new direction and lying along a new line. The swing is paced by the outer file — the corner man farthest from the hinge jogs his arc while the inner files slow proportionally — so a wide or deep block takes correspondingly longer to come around. Use it to refuse a flank, to swing a wing forward and take an enemy line in the side, or to change the front an entire wing presents without breaking its order.

Moving wheel (cavalry only). A mounted regiment doesn’t need to halt for any of the standing maneuvers above. Give a cavalry unit a move order sharply off its current heading — forward-oblique, to a flank, or to the rear, at any angle, even one that would otherwise call for a full about-face — and it gallops through the turn in a single continuous wheel: the hinge advances at the unit’s own march pace the whole swing while the outer files sweep faster to keep the line’s shape, then it rides straight into the march with no halt at either end. A foot regiment given the identical order still takes the standing route above — an about-face, an about-face plus flank pivot, or a lateral pivot, depending on the angle — since only cavalry is fast and maneuverable enough to carve the turn without breaking stride. The same choice applies whether the order comes from your own click or from an AI subcommander’s directive (a “hold this ground” / “cover this flank” order): a subcommander leading a cavalry group from the front can decompose its own directive into the same moving wheel followed by a forward march.

Countermarch (exelismos, Shift+V / Ctrl+V / Ctrl+Shift+V). All three variants share the about-face’s V key, the same way Shift+O shares O with the anti-cavalry square — every plain letter hotkey is already claimed elsewhere. Unlike the conversio (every man turns in place, keeping his own spot) or the wheel (a hinge swing on one flank), a countermarch reverses which end of the line faces the enemy by marching files through and around each other: the files countermarch past their own file-mates so a genuine full-strength rank always ends up leading, instead of whichever partial or depleted rank happened to be trailing after a plain about-face. The three historical variants (Asclepiodotus, Tactica 10; Aelian, Tactica Theoria 27–28) differ only in where the whole regiment ends up:

  • Shift+V Choral/Persian — reverses facing on the very ground the unit already stands on, the in-place counterpart to the conversio.
  • Ctrl+V Macedonian — reverses facing and advances, roughly one file’s depth onto new ground. Use it to meet a fresh threat that has appeared behind the line.
  • Ctrl+Shift+V Laconian — reverses facing and withdraws, roughly one file’s depth back. A controlled disengagement that cedes ground instead of gaining it.

All four drills fire from a standstill only: a unit that is marching, fighting, or already mid-maneuver ignores the key press. While one runs, the info panel reports it (“About-facing”, “Quarter-turning”, “Wheeling”, “Countermarching (Macedonian)”), and any fresh order cancels the drill where it stands.

Zoom in to see the soldiers. From the usual overview each regiment is a block of small marks. Zoom in far enough and those marks become individual figures, and each type carries its own gear — spearmen shoulder a spear, archers carry a bow, other foot raise a shield, and cavalry ride a mount that faces the regiment’s march. So you can watch the ranks as people, not dots, and still read who is who. A small white facing pip on each figure shows its exact facing — the figure itself only mirrors left/right, so the pip is what lets a maneuver’s precise turn (a quarter-turn, say) read correctly even at this zoom, not just a coarse left/right flip. Zoom back out and they collapse to the legible marks again — each mark points the way its soldier faces, so you can read a regiment’s facing at a glance even from the overview. The mark shape tells you the type, too: a dart for spearmen, a kite for archers, and an arrow-pointer for other foot and cavalry. All three are compact directional glyphs, so a rank reads as arrows whichever way it faces rather than smearing into bars.

Reading distances. A small semi-translucent scale bar sits in the bottom-right corner, labelled with the real metre distance it spans (e.g. “50 m”) — it rescales as you zoom, so you can always judge range and reach at a glance. Toggle it off in ☰ Menu → Distance legend if you’d rather not see it.

Order stances. Beyond plain move/attack you can arm a stance with a hotkey before you click; a coloured cursor shows which is active, and Esc clears it.

  • Hold (H) — stand ground; don’t chase a detected enemy.
  • Attack flank (F) / Attack rear (R) — maneuver to strike the enemy’s side or back, where hits land far harder (see Tactics).
  • Skirmish (K) — a ranged unit keeps its distance, backing off when a threat closes instead of standing to fire.
  • Support (G) — right-click a friendly to guard it: the unit shadows that ally and engages anything that threatens it.
  • Cycle charge (J) — cavalry charge the target, pull back to a standoff, and charge again, so each run lands a fresh momentum hit instead of grinding once the charge is spent (see Tactics).
  • Sweep routers (,) — the unit prioritizes a routing (fleeing) enemy over a closer enemy that’s still standing and fighting, chasing down the break before mopping up the line (see Tactics).
  • Roll the line (;) — once the current target routs or is destroyed, the unit automatically re-targets the next-closest enemy still fighting instead of standing idle or chasing a broken foe, so a single attack order can carry a unit down a whole enemy line (see Tactics).
  • Pin down (.) — stall an enemy confronted head-on: the unit swings less often than normal, but each swing’s follow-through briefly leaves it more exposed to a hit — a genuine tradeoff, not a pure defensive buff (see Tactics).
  • All-out attack (’) — trade defense for offense: the unit hits harder but is itself easier to hit, so both sides in the exchange take more casualties than a fair fight would (see Tactics).
  • Chase (\) — relentless pursuit: the unit never takes the normal disengage exit a plain move order gives it, so it keeps closing on and re-engaging the same fleeing or repositioning enemy instead of breaking off (see Tactics).
  • Wedge charge (-) — trade defense and stamina for a faster approach: the unit closes the distance quicker, but is itself easier to hit and tires faster while fighting (see Tactics).
  • Knockback focus (=) — trade damage for a much bigger, more probable push-back. Hold Shift when arming or issuing it to push the struck enemy much further back instead of just clearing the battle line (see Tactics).

Pressing a stance key alone only arms it for your next move/attack order — it doesn’t touch a unit that’s already standing still. To change a selected unit’s stance right where it stands, with no move needed, hold Ctrl while pressing the stance key; a toast confirms the write. Arming a new stance before Shift+right-clicking to queue a waypoint stamps that stance onto just the newly queued leg — it takes effect once the unit’s route reaches that leg, without touching whatever stance is already active on the leg the unit is currently marching. Separately, I toggles each selected unit’s rank relief (discipline) mode — the on-by-default knob behind rank cycling (see Tactics) — with its own confirming toast.

Relieve a fighting friendly (line relief). Right-click an engaged friendly outside your selection to order a relief: the fresh unit advances into the fight and the tired one peels back toward its own rear to rest. The two regiments pass through each other during the swap — the reliever takes over the melee, and the relieved unit’s retreat is a real move order, so holding Space shows it like any other march. Any fresh order to the reliever cancels the swap where it stands. Relief is how you win a long grind — see Tactics.

Relieve a whole tired line at once (passage of lines). Select every fresh unit in your reserve, switch the group-order mode to Distributed (X or the control bar), then right-click any one engaged unit in the tired line. Instead of every reliever swapping with just the unit you clicked, each pairs with its own nearby engaged friendly — the whole reserve passes through the whole tired line in one order. See Tactics for how the pairing is chosen.

Press T to cycle a selected unit’s formation — Tight closes ranks for better missile and charge defence, Loose spreads out, and Square forms the anti-cavalry ring described below. Every unit can cycle through all four; each type just starts at a different one — spearmen deploy Tight (locked shields against a charge), archers deploy Loose (room to fire), and sword-armed infantry and cavalry deploy at the plain Normal combat order.

The grid each density mode scales is itself per-type: foot regiments stand at close order (about half a metre per man on both axes), while a cavalry regiment’s grid is far roomier — about a metre between files and three metres between ranks, the riding room a horse needs side-to-side and nose-to-tail. A mounted regiment therefore occupies several times the ground of a foot block with the same headcount, and its deep column takes visibly longer to wheel or pivot.

Square: orbis and schiltron (anti-cavalry rings). Square forms a hollow square: the soldiers face spears and shields outward on every side, so the unit has no weak flank or rear — an attack from any direction lands as if it were frontal, and a cavalry charge into the ring is braced (and backfires) no matter which side it hits. The ring is visible at a glance from the individual soldiers’ own outward-facing marks around the block’s perimeter — no separate overlay drawn on top. The price is mobility (the ring crawls) and offence (it hits softer while hunkered), so square up before the horse arrives and drop back to Normal once the threat is gone. There are two variants of the ring, trading off differently:

  • Orbis — the last-stand ring. Its offence and charge-bracing sit at the square’s baseline, and it holds its nerve better than any other stance: casualties erode its morale more slowly, so a surrounded unit making a final stand is less likely to break outright. Because it’s a reaction you want to trigger fast, O (for orbis) jumps a unit straight to Square without cycling through Tight and Loose — press it again to drop back to Normal — and the control bar’s Square (Orbis) button does the same with one click.
  • Schiltron — the cavalry specialist. A denser hedge of levelled spears braces a charge harder than the orbis does, so cavalry that rides into a schiltron comes off worse than riding into an orbis, at the cost of hitting even softer in return. It carries no morale bonus — unlike orbis this isn’t a last-stand stance, just the hard anti-cavalry counter. Shift + O jumps a unit straight to Schiltron — press it again to drop back to Normal — and the control bar’s Schiltron button does the same.

Shielded close-order stances. Two heavier stances build on Tight’s locked-shield density, chosen from the control-bar Formation menu:

  • Shield Wall — shields locked edge-to-edge in a static line. It sharply cuts frontal missile and melee damage, but only to the front: a shot or blow into the flank or rear bypasses the wall entirely. The wall barely moves, so it is a holding stance — plant it to anchor a line, not to advance.
  • Testudo — shields locked front, sides, and overhead. It blunts missile fire from every direction, the strongest arrow cover in the game, but the men are packed head-down under their shields and fight weakly, and it is the slowest stance of all. Use it to walk a unit across open ground under heavy archery, then drop back to a fighting formation before you close.

Both stances show their own footprint on the map, so you can read a unit’s stance directly from how the soldiers stand: a shield wall packs into a tight edge-to-edge line, and a testudo packs into a dense, deep block (the turtle) — no separate schematic drawn on top, just the real, physical formation geometry.

Resize the line. A regiment normally forms up at a sensible width, but you can reshape it: press ] to widen the line by one file (shallower, more men fighting at the front) or [ to narrow it (deeper, fewer up front but more staying power). With a single unit selected, two small grips appear on its flanks — drag a grip in or out to set the width directly, with a live preview of the new file count. The drag anchors the far flank: only the dragged edge moves, like resizing a window by its edge, so you can extend a line toward the flank you care about while the other edge holds its ground (the [ / ] keys stay symmetric about the centre). The width holds as the unit takes casualties, and shows in the info panel.

File-major reform (default File-major). By default, each soldier keeps a persistent file (column) assignment: when a file-mate falls, only the survivors deeper in that SAME file step forward to close the gap, and every other file stands untouched — a mauled unit’s rear reads as jagged (one column can run deeper than its neighbour) rather than a single evenly-shrinking rear rank. Click the File-major reform button in the info panel to cycle to Row-major, the historical behavior instead: the whole grid recomputes fresh from the live headcount every tick, so survivors close up toward the centre file by file and can shuffle sideways between files as the block re-knits itself (see Tactics). A third click cycles to Auto, which picks File-major or Row-major for you from the unit’s own discipline: a disciplined, professional regiment reflows File-major, an undisciplined mob reflows Row-major — useful for a mixed army where you’d otherwise have to set each unit by hand to match its own training.

File-doubling (explicatio and duplicatio). Where [ / ] nudge the width one file at a time, these two drill maneuvers reshape it by a whole factor at once. Press B for explicatio: each file splits and the rear half steps out to the side, so the frontage doubles and the depth halves — a column unfolds into a broad line that brings more men to the front, good for overlapping a flank. Press N for duplicatio: alternate files tuck in behind their neighbours, so the frontage halves and the depth doubles — a wide line folds into a deep, narrow column, good for punching through or squeezing down a lane. Nobody teleports: the soldiers walk to their new slots, so the reshape takes a moment to settle. Explicatio can’t widen past a single rank, and duplicatio bottoms out at one file.

Anchored (asymmetric) explicatio. The plain explicatio above widens symmetrically about the unit’s own centre — both flanks step outward by the same amount. Press Shift+B to widen instead while holding the right flank fixed, or Ctrl+B to hold the left flank fixed: the whole widen lands on the opposite side, so the unit’s position on the field shifts rather than staying centred. Useful when a flank must stay pinned to terrain or a neighbouring unit while the line still needs to broaden.

Deploy with a drag (form up). With a single unit selected, right-click and drag across open ground to lay out where it should stand: the start of the drag is the unit’s left flank, the end is its right flank. The unit marches over and pivots to face the line — the drag sets its position, width, and facing in one gesture, with a live preview of the front line and a forward arrow. A short right-click (no drag) is still a plain move.

Deploy several units at once. Select multiple units first, then drag the same flank line: it’s split into one contiguous slice per unit, with a small gap between them. The split can hold either depth (front-to-back ranks) or width (frontage) equal across the units, and can measure “equal” either by space (physical world units, accounting for each unit’s own density) or by count (a plain rank/file number, blind to density) — four combinations in all:

  • Equal depth (space) — the default. Every regiment forms up to the same physical depth: a tightly-packed unit fits more ranks into that depth than a loosely-spread one, so the whole front reads as one uniform wall front-to-back.
  • Equal depth (count) — every regiment gets the same number of ranks regardless of density, so a loosely-spread unit ends up physically deeper than a tightly-packed one at the same rank count.
  • Equal width (space) — every unit takes the same physical frontage; a smaller unit just stacks deeper to fit its slice.
  • Equal width (count) — every unit gets the same file count (soldiers per rank), so physical frontage differs when density does.

For a same-density group (every selected unit in the same stance) the space and count variants of a given dimension produce identical results — the distinction only matters when you drag a mixed group (e.g. a Tight unit alongside a Loose one). Press Y to cycle through the enabled modes and Shift+Y to cycle backward; the choice you make in ☰ Menu → Form-up sets the default each battle starts with, and which modes the Y-key cycles through. By default the units fill the line left-to-right by where they already stand, so each marches to the nearest slice without crossing a neighbour; hold Shift while you drag to place them in selection order instead (the first unit you picked takes the left flank). The preview shows every unit’s slice, file count, and the active split mode before you release.

Checkerboard (quincunx). A fifth split mode, off by default (enable it in ☰ Menu → Form-up): instead of one straight line, your selection alternates into two staggered rows — the historical Roman acies triplex arrangement. The 1st, 3rd, 5th, … units (by field position) form the front row along the dragged line, with wider gaps between them (roughly each unit’s own frontage, not the ordinary small seam). The 2nd, 4th, … units form a rear row, each centred laterally on one of the front row’s gaps and standing a short distance behind it. See tactics: acies triplex for why you’d want this.

Echelon (oblique order). Two more split modes, off by default (enable Echelon (right leads) or Echelon (left leads) in ☰ Menu → Form-up): instead of one straight rank, your selection staggers into a diagonal, one flank advanced and the other held back. Right leads puts the unit nearest your drag’s right flank furthest forward and the left-flank unit furthest back; left leads mirrors it. Every unit in between steps evenly between the two ends. See tactics: echelon (oblique order) for when you’d want a refused flank instead of a solid line.

Survey all orders (hold Space). Holding Space overlays every unit’s current order on the battlefield: a dashed green line to each move destination, a red line to each attack target, and a teal link for support duties. At the end of each move line, a ghost formation (faint green dots) shows exactly where each individual soldier will stand when the regiment finishes its march — so you can check whether units will fit, overlap, or leave a gap before they arrive. The ghost reads the destination stance’s actual shape, so a unit marching in Square (or Schiltron) previews as the real hollow-square footprint, not the wide line the other formations use.

Each order line also carries a distance label at its midpoint, in the same metres the scale bar uses — the remaining march for a move (the full route, summed over any waypoints), or the range to the target for an attack or support link. The number counts down as the unit closes. Toggle the labels off in ☰ Menu → Order distance labels (on by default).

The battle loop

Here is the cycle in practice, shown with a cavalry charge:

  1. Select — left-click a unit, or drag a box to grab several at once. The info panel fills with the selected unit’s stats, including a flavor Commander rank (Centurion, Pilus Prior, Decurion, …) drawn from the real Roman officer hierarchy — cosmetic only, it has no effect on combat. Below the headline stats sit the live lines — each man’s hit points (the living average, its spread, and the type’s full-health value), the ordered gait with the unit’s own pace for it, and the block’s live mean speed and how fast that is changing — updating live as the unit marches and fights. The unit’s static characteristics sit below them behind a ▸ Characteristics toggle, folded up by default (click to expand): attack and defense; the armor type with its protection and weight (a legionary’s mail, a spearman’s linothorax, an archer’s padded tunic, a cavalryman’s scale shirt); each man’s body mass in kilograms; the weapon and shield types with their reach, lethality, block, and arc; and the mount (a cavalry regiment’s warhorse, with its own mass in kilograms and its pace — the horse’s bulk behind every charge). Every line carries a single stat, so the panel reads as one narrow top-to-bottom column. The panel’s Order line shows the unit’s current order as a small tree when it’s a composite maneuver (a move-to-the-rear’s turn-then-march, say): each step is indented under the maneuver it belongs to, the step actually running is highlighted, and a triangle toggle expands or collapses the steps. A simple order with no sub-steps still shows as the single line it always has.
  2. Order — right-click to send them. Move them onto an enemy’s flank, or right-click the enemy directly to attack. There is a brief reaction delay (~0.5 s, faster for cavalry) before units execute — orders aren’t instant.
  3. Watch the bars — each unit flies a small regimental flag in its team colour and shows a strength bar that drops as it takes casualties. The flag is a click target too: clicking it selects the unit, just like clicking the block. Morale matters as much as numbers (see Tactics).
  4. Navigate terrain — the field has two terrain patches: a dark-green forest (slows movement to 60%) on the left flank, and a tan hill (impassable) on the right. Units route around the hill automatically, whether under your orders or fleeing a rout — and a routing unit boxed in with no way around stands to fight instead. See Tactics → Terrain.
  5. Exploit the break — when a unit routs, it flees (its flag fading) and stops fighting for now. Routs spread to nearby allies, so a single break can cascade. A fugitive that breaks contact can rally and reform back under your control at low morale, so chase down a broken enemy to finish it before it recovers — and give your own routers room to escape and rally.
  6. Win — destroy the entire enemy army (every unit dead, or fled clear of the map) and a Victory overlay appears, with Fight Again to restart. A routing enemy isn’t beaten yet: a “broken” one may yet rally, and even a “shattered” one (its nerve gone for good) still has to escape or be run down before it’s actually off the field.

Parade Ground

Select Parade Ground from the Main Menu for a solo drill: your own army deploys as usual, but no enemy spawns and the battle never auto-ends. Use it to freely rehearse formations, maneuvers, and orders without an opponent forcing the pace. Leave with ☰ Menu → Quit to Main Menu when you’re done — there’s no victory condition to trigger it for you.

All-Teams Control

Select All-Teams Control from the Main Menu for a real two-army battle where you command both sides directly — unlike Parade Ground, the enemy army still deploys as normal, but no AI ever issues it orders. Select and command either side’s units the same way you would your own; a right-click targets the OTHER side relative to whatever you currently have selected, so the same controls work switching back and forth between armies. Useful for rehearsing or verifying combat mechanics deterministically, without an opponent’s own AI decisions in the way.

Campaign Map

Sparta includes a turn-based campaign layer on top of its tactical battles. Select a scenario from the Main Menu to open the campaign map.

On your turn:

  1. Click one of your provinces (highlighted when you have an army there).
  2. Click an adjacent province to move, or attack an enemy one if you are at war.
  3. Press End Turn when you are done. Each AI faction takes its turn automatically.

Diplomacy: the right-hand panel lists every surviving rival. Click Declare War or Sue for Peace to change your stance. Suing for peace sets a truce that prevents re-declaring war for a few turns.

Rulers and AI personality: each faction is led by a named ruler whose trait shapes its diplomacy. The turn banner at top-left names the current faction’s ruler in parentheses (for example, Rome’s banner reads “Rome (Julius Caesar)”).

Province counts appear in the standings line below the banner. Own every province to win the campaign.

Recording & replays

Every battle is recorded automatically — a ● REC indicator shows top-center. When a battle ends it’s saved. Leaving early instead — Quit to Main Menu or Return to Campaign, including from a never-ending Parade Ground drill — offers a Save Replay? prompt first, so a mid-battle recording isn’t silently lost. Once a battle’s saved (either way), you can:

  • Watch Replay — re-run the battle you just played (▶ REPLAY). You can still pan and inspect units during playback, but can’t issue new orders.
  • Load Replay — open any earlier saved battle from a file picker (top-right, also on the end screen).
  • Fight Again — start a fresh, newly-recorded battle.

See Replays for how the deterministic system works and why the logs are tiny.

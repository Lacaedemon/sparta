Tactics That Matter

Flanking, morale and routing, the unit triangle, terrain, and disengaging.
Published

Last updated: 2026-07-23

Numbers aren’t everything. Sparta’s battles are decided by where units are and which way they face — flanking, morale, and the rock-paper-scissors of unit types turn an even fight lopsided.

Flanking

Hitting a unit from the side or rear deals far more damage and morale loss than a frontal clash:

Maneuver behind the enemy line. A regiment pinned frontally by a screen while your cavalry hits its rear will melt — and start a rout that spreads.

The enemy watches its own flanks too. An AI-controlled unit that finds a live threat closing on its side or rear turns to meet it — it won’t stand fighting head-on while your cavalry lines up an unanswered flank strike. Catching the enemy still costs real setup: bring the second threat into contact before it reacts, or pin the front so the flanking unit has nowhere better to turn.

Morale & routing

Units don’t fight to the last soldier. When a unit loses enough soldiers or takes flank/rear hits, it routs — it flees (its flag fading) toward its own back edge and stops fighting for now. A routing unit can flee past the field’s visible edge into a margin beyond it before it’s gone for good, staying on the field — and still fightable — for as long as it’s plausibly still in view.

  • Routs are contagious. A nearby ally watching a unit break is more likely to break too. One collapse can cascade down a line.
  • This makes morale a weapon: you can win by breaking the enemy’s will before you’ve killed even half their soldiers.
  • A routing unit is still fair game. Routing doesn’t grant immunity — a fleeing unit can still be struck and run down, chased as relentlessly as your pursuer can manage. So chase a broken enemy down to finish it, rather than assuming it’s out of the fight.
  • A “broken” unit can rally if nothing catches it. As it flees, its nerve steadies — morale climbs back toward a baseline, fast at first and levelling off. If it breaks contact (no enemy close enough to keep hitting it) and still fields enough men, its recovering morale carries it over the rally threshold and it returns to your control, ready to fight again.
  • Keep pressing it and it “shatters” instead — still in contact when its time runs out, or gutted past reforming. A shattered unit does NOT vanish: it stays on the field, still routing, but can never again recover morale or rally. From there it either escapes (flees clear of the map, gone but got away) or is run down and annihilated (soldiers reduced to zero) before it can.
  • A routing unit paths around impassable terrain too — fleeing is no longer a straight line toward the back edge; a router detours around a hill in its way the same as an ordered move does. If every escape direction is blocked, it gives up fleeing and stands to fight instead of uselessly running in place against the obstacle.
  • The battle isn’t over while a side is only routing. A team is beaten only once its last unit is truly gone (escaped, annihilated, or otherwise removed), not while its final unit is merely routing. Even a lone survivor that breaks can flee, rally, and fight on — so press a rout home rather than assuming a broken last unit is a win.

Rank cycling (trained units)

A well-trained melee unit periodically rotates its ranks — the tired front line steps back and a fresh rank takes its place, signalled by a whistle. Cycling slows fatigue buildup (by up to half at full training) and lets the unit recover morale even while still fighting. The better trained the unit, the more often it cycles. Ranged units don’t cycle.

Fatigue builds at a realistic pace — sustained melee wears a unit down over minutes, not seconds, and resting in the rear restores it just as gradually. So relief is a deliberate decision for a prolonged fight, not a frantic every-few-seconds chore.

Training therefore pays off in a long grind: a drilled regiment wears down a green one of equal numbers, because the green unit tires and wavers first. Rank cycling buys TIME, not immunity: the recovery it grants fades as the regiment thins out (fewer fresh files left to rotate to the front) and stops once it’s badly bloodied. So a veteran unit facing the same sustained pressure still routs eventually — it just takes noticeably longer and costs the attacker more losses to break it than a green unit under the same attack.

Rank relief (discipline toggle). Rank cycling is on by default for every unit, but you can switch it off with I (see How to play for the control). A unit with rank relief off holds its ranks fixed — no fatigue relief, no in-fight morale recovery from cycling — which trades away that endurance edge for a line that never opens the small gaps a whistled rotation briefly creates. Leave it on for a grinding attritional fight; consider switching it off only when you’d rather keep the front rank locked in place than let it step back mid-melee.

Line relief

Rank cycling rotates soldiers within a regiment; line relief rotates whole regiments. Right-click an engaged friendly with a fresh unit selected: the fresh unit advances through its tired ally and takes over the melee, and the relieved regiment falls back toward its own rear — where fatigue drains away and morale recovers (see How to play for the controls).

  • The swap is a pass-through. For its duration the two friendly regiments may interpenetrate, so the line never opens a gap for the enemy to pour into while the fresh unit works its way forward.
  • The retreat is an order like any other. The relieved unit falls back under a normal move order (hold Space to see it), and you can redirect it — to a safer rest spot, or straight to another fight if you must.
  • Interrupting the reliever cancels the swap. The swap lives on the reliever’s relief order, so a fresh order mid-pass ends it where it stands and the pair stops ignoring each other’s bodies. Avoid changing your mind while the two regiments still overlap.
  • Expect to bleed a little on the way out. The enemy keeps swinging while the retiring ranks are in reach, so pulling out of contact costs a few men — far fewer than staying until the regiment breaks.

Rotate before the break, not after: a tired regiment fights measurably worse (see Stamina) and routs sooner, and pulling it out while it still holds banks a rested unit for the next push.

Passage of lines

Line relief swaps one pair of regiments at a time by default — every fresh unit you order onto an engaged friendly swaps with (or, past the first, piles onto the fight of) that ONE unit you clicked. When a whole tired line needs relieving at once, select every fresh unit in your reserve, switch the group-order mode to Distributed (X, or the Spread button — see How to play for the control), then right-click any one engaged unit in the tired line. Each fresh unit pairs with its OWN nearby engaged friendly instead of all of them swapping with the single unit clicked, so the whole reserve passes through the whole tired line in one order — Polybius’s triplex acies, a fresh line relieving a spent one through gaps opened in the line behind it.

  • The candidate pool is engaged friendlies only. A reliever never swaps with an idle or already-routing unit just to keep busy — if fewer of your tired line are actually fighting than you have fresh units, the extras fall back to piling onto a fight instead of manufacturing a swap.
  • Pairing is sorted by proximity to the unit you clicked, not to each reliever’s own position. With reserves lined up abreast of a tired line, this usually pairs each fresh unit with roughly the ally in front of it, but it can cross — the same distribution rule distributed attack already uses for spreading a multi-unit attack across several enemies.
  • Every pairing is a full line relief, with the same pass-through exemption, the same real retreat order, and the same “expect to bleed a little on the way out” cost described above — distributed relief only changes how the targets are assigned, not how any one swap behaves.

Acies triplex

The Roman acies triplex deployed infantry in three lines — hastati, principes, triarii — with gaps between maniples in one line roughly matching their own frontage width, and the line behind offset to cover those gaps: a checkerboard (quincunx) pattern. Passage of lines (above) builds the tactical half — swapping a whole tired line for a fresh one through gaps. Checkerboard form-up builds the spatial half: a fifth multi-unit form-up split mode (off by default; enable it in ☰ Menu → Form-up — Checkerboard (quincunx), or cycle to it with Y once enabled) that lays your selection out as two staggered rows instead of one straight line. See How to play: Controls (the “Deploy several units at once” entry) for the drag mechanics.

This is a one-shot layout, not a standing formation — a unit dragged into a checkerboard arrangement doesn’t remember “I’m line 2” the way it remembers its stance or formation mode. Nothing yet models the historical system’s chief weakness either: a gapped, multi-line formation trades frontage flexibility for flank exposure, so a checkerboard-formed army isn’t currently more vulnerable to flanking than a solid line. See docs/acies-triplex-design.md in the project repository for the full design note and what’s tracked as follow-up work.

Echelon (oblique order)

Every form-up split covered so far keeps the whole selection on one straight rank. Echelon breaks that on purpose: it staggers your line into a diagonal, one flank stepped forward and the other held back. Caesar deployed this way at times in the Gallic War, and Frederick the Great refined it into his signature oblique order at Leuthen — weight one flank, refuse the other, and let the enemy fight an uneven battle instead of a fair one.

Two more multi-unit form-up split modes carry it, both off by default (enable one in ☰ Menu → Form-up, or cycle to it with Y once enabled):

  • Echelon (right leads) — the unit nearest your drag’s right flank advances furthest, the left-flank unit trails furthest back, and everyone in between steps evenly across the gap. Concentrate the leading (right) flank on a weak point in the enemy line while the trailing (left) flank stays out of easy reach.
  • Echelon (left leads) — the mirror image: the left flank leads, the right flank refuses.

See How to play: Controls (the “Deploy several units at once” entry) for the drag mechanics — echelon reuses the exact same box-select-then-drag gesture every other split mode does, just with a staggered depth instead of one flat line.

Like checkerboard, this is a one-shot layout, not a standing formation: a unit dragged into an echelon doesn’t remember which flank it led from, so a later reform or reshape doesn’t preserve the stagger. And nothing currently models the tactical risk a real refused flank carries either — the trailing units aren’t rendered any more exposed to being turned than a straight line would be, so the payoff (concentrating force where you choose) isn’t yet balanced by the historical downside (a flank that isn’t in the fight yet is a flank an enemy could exploit before it arrives).

Stamina

Every soldier carries a stamina pool that drains through combat and refills at rest. Each blow thrown costs stamina; throwing blows costs more than absorbing them. Rising from the ground after being knocked down costs a burst extra. Low stamina lowers the soldier’s condition factor, which scales both attack output and defence — a tired soldier hits softer and is easier to hit.

So a unit that has been grinding at the front for a while is measurably weaker than one that just arrived fresh. Rank cycling (above) helps by rotating tired soldiers to the rear to recover; simply pulling a unit out of melee and letting it rest works too.

Prone / knockdown

A blow that delivers enough force can knock a soldier off their feet. A prone soldier has no active defence — only their armour absorbs incoming hits — and must spend both time (about two seconds) and stamina to get back up.

Visually, prone soldiers stand out:

  • At normal zoom (mark LOD): a prone soldier’s mark is flattened into a wide horizontal sliver so it reads clearly as “lying flat” among the standing marks.
  • Zoomed in (figure LOD): the figure silhouette is rotated 90° to show a person lying on their side.

Engaged lines resist knockdown. A unit that is actively locked in melee (and not in Skirmish stance) braces automatically, raising the impulse threshold needed to knock a soldier flat. A skirmishing or unengaged unit does not brace and is easier to topple. A deeper file also helps — more soldiers absorbing the same front-rank pressure means fewer individual soldiers pushed past the knockdown threshold.

A cluster of prone soldiers in the front rank is a warning sign: your line is tired or outmatched there, and each fallen body briefly opens a gap in your defence before they can rise.

Line width (frontage)

You set how wide each regiment fights — [ and ] step the width by a file symmetrically, or drag a selected unit’s flank grips (the drag anchors the far flank, so the line extends toward the grip you pull — handy for stretching a line toward the flank you need to cover without giving ground on the other side). The same soldiers spread across a different shape:

  • Wide and shallow puts more men in the front rank, so more of them are swinging at once — good for overlapping an enemy’s flank or covering ground so a foe can’t slip around your line.
  • Narrow and deep stacks more ranks behind a thinner front. Fewer fight at any moment, but the column has more bodies to feed forward as the front falls — it holds a chokepoint or punches through one spot rather than spreading thin.

A wider line is easier to outlast in depth and quicker to crumble once it bends; a deeper one trades frontage for staying power. A width you’ve set by hand (the bracket keys or a flank drag) holds exactly as you left it — the game never overrides your explicit choice.

Close the ranks. Left on auto, though, a regiment that’s lost half its strength narrows its own frontage one notch, trading width for depth so the survivors reform into a smaller, still-solid block rather than stretching a nearly-spent line across its original width. It widens back out again if the regiment claws back above about two-thirds strength (reinforcement, a relief, a lull in the fighting) — but not the instant it edges past halfway, so a unit trading casualties right around the line doesn’t flicker between shapes fight to fight.

File-doubling for a fast reshape. The [ / ] steps are fine for fine-tuning, but two drill maneuvers swap the whole shape in one press. B (explicatio) doubles the frontage and halves the depth — a column unfolds into a wide line to overlap a flank or plug a gap. N (duplicatio) does the reverse: it halves the frontage and doubles the depth, folding a broad line into a deep column so you can squeeze down a lane or mass depth to punch through one point. The men walk to their new slots rather than snapping, so there’s a beat of disorder while the block reshapes — don’t start the maneuver in contact, and give it room before the enemy arrives. A natural rhythm is duplicatio to march a column through a chokepoint, then explicatio to deploy back into line on the far side.

Anchored explicatio for a pinned flank. The plain explicatio widens about the unit’s own centre, so both flanks drift when the frontage doubles — fine in the open, but a problem when one flank is braced against a hill, a river, or a neighbouring unit that can’t shift with it. Shift+B widens while holding the right flank fixed; Ctrl+B holds the left. The whole widen lands on the free flank instead, so the pinned side never moves and the line still gets the extra frontage where there’s room for it.

How a line reforms after losses. By default (File-major, the unit settings panel’s File-major reform button), every soldier holds a persistent file (column) assignment: when a man falls, only the survivors behind him in his OWN file step forward to close that one gap, and every other file is untouched — nobody switches columns just because a neighbouring file took a hit. A mauled unit’s rear reads as jagged, not uniform: a hard-hit file can run noticeably shallower than one that’s taken no losses at all, side by side in the same rank of survivors. The front rank still keeps its full width for as long as any file has a man left to hold it, and the block never bulges wider than the frontage you set — only the depth, file by file, tells you where the losses landed.

Click the button through to Row-major for the historical behavior instead: the whole grid recomputes fresh from the live headcount every tick, so the file behind each fighter feeds men forward to hold the contact line and the depth shrinks rank by rank, uniformly. When the rearmost rank is left short-handed, its survivors close up toward the centre — the wings shorten first while the middle files stay deepest — rather than leaving a ragged, gap-toothed back rank, but a soldier’s own file assignment isn’t stable: it can shift sideways as the whole block re-knits itself around the casualty.

A third click cycles to Auto, which resolves to File-major or Row-major per unit from its own disciplined flag rather than a fixed choice — a disciplined, professional regiment reflows File-major (it holds its ranks under loss), an undisciplined mob reflows Row-major (it just closes up whatever gap the loss opens). Useful for a mixed army: set the whole selection to Auto once, and each unit reforms the way its own training would suggest, rather than picking File-major or Row-major by hand per unit.

Deploy a whole battle line at once. Select several regiments and drag one flank line (right-click and drag): the line splits among them. By default it splits to equal depth (space), so every regiment forms up to the same physical depth — a uniform front where bigger (or denser) units just take a wider slice. That keeps your whole line’s staying power even, with no thin spot for the enemy to punch through. Press Y (or Shift + Y to cycle the other way) to switch modes — equal width when you’d rather every unit cover the same frontage (small units stack deeper), or one of the count-based variants when you want density differences ignored entirely (see Deploy several units at once in How to Play for what each of the four modes holds equal). They sort left-to-right by where they already stand, so nobody crosses the field to reach a slot — a fast way to dress a ragged group into one clean front before the enemy closes. Hold Shift to place them in selection order instead when you want a specific regiment on a specific flank.

Use ☰ Menu → Form-up to configure the split: the upper radio buttons set the mode each battle starts in; the lower checkboxes (“Y-key cycles through…”) control which modes Y steps between mid-battle. Uncheck a mode to skip it entirely from the cycle.

The unit triangle (rock-paper-scissors)

Three roles, each strong against one and weak to another:

🗡️ Infantry

The reliable line. No special bonus — your anvil to pin the enemy in place while something else does the killing.

🐎 Cavalry

Fast — they charge at a hard canter (~8.5 m/s, far quicker than any foot unit’s advance) — with a charge bonus that grows with closing speed. Devastating into a flank or rear, but blunted when it charges into braced spears.

🛡️ Spearmen

Anti-cavalry (marked with a spear). They blunt the cavalry charge, so use them to screen your line against enemy horse. Their long spears also out-reach shorter weapons, landing the first blows as lines close (see below).

The loop: cavalry beats exposed flanks → spearmen beat cavalry → infantry pin so your cavalry can get around to those flanks. Read the enemy’s screen before you commit your horse.

Formation stances

Press T to cycle a unit’s density — Normal, Tight (closed ranks: better missile and charge defence), Loose (open order: room to fire), and Square (the anti-cavalry ring, covered below). Shift + T cycles the other way, handy for stepping back one density without looping all the way round. Two heavier shielded close-order stances build on Tight’s locked-shield density and are set from the control-bar Formation menu or their own direct keys.

  • Shield Wall (L) — shields locked edge-to-edge, a static line. It sharply cuts the missile and melee damage a unit takes from the front, but only from the front: a shot or blow into the flank or rear ignores the wall. The wall barely moves, so it is a holding stance. Anchor a flank or a chokepoint with it and let the enemy break on the front — but keep its flanks screened, because that is where it is soft.
  • Testudo (U) — shields locked front, sides, and overhead: the strongest missile cover in the game, and it works from every direction. The cost is that the men fight weakly (packed head-down under their shields) and move slowest of all. It is an arrow-crossing stance, not a fighting one: walk a unit through a beaten zone of archery in testudo, then drop to Tight or Normal before it reaches melee.

Both shielded stances still brace against a cavalry charge the way Tight does, so neither is helpless if horse reaches them — but only spearmen actually turn a charge back. Because both are slow, commit them deliberately: a testudo caught in the open by melee, or a shield wall taken in the flank, is in trouble.

Form square against cavalry

When horse threatens a unit’s flank or rear and you have no spear screen to spare, form a hollow square at once: O (for orbis) jumps straight to the Square stance, and T also reaches it by cycling through Tight and Loose. The soldiers face outward on every side, so the unit presents no weak facing: an attack from any direction lands as if it were frontal (the usual ×1.5 flank / ×2 rear bonus is gone), and a cavalry charge into the ring is braced and backfires whether it hits the front, a flank, or the rear. There is no open side for the horse to exploit. The soldiers’ own outward-facing marks around the block’s perimeter make the stance easy to spot on the field — no separate ring overlay drawn on top.

The brace is physical, not a flat damage penalty. Watch a charging cavalry regiment hit a set square: its riders’ momentum is genuinely absorbed by the individual bodies it collides with, not waved through by a rule, so the charge visibly slows and settles at the ring instead of riding on through it.

Square is a defensive commitment, not a free upgrade. While it holds, the unit crawls (it can’t charge out) and hits softer (reduced offensive output). So:

The AI squares up on its own. An AI-controlled spear unit forms Square the moment enemy cavalry closes on it, with no player prompting it — the same reaction a disciplined human commander would take. Charging AI spearmen in the open only works if the cavalry arrives before the square finishes forming.

  • Square up early — before the charge lands, not as it connects. The mobility penalty means a late square may not finish forming in time.
  • Drop back to Normal the moment the horse is beaten off or pulls away, or the offence and mobility penalties will cost you against enemy foot.
  • It’s a fallback, not a replacement for spearmen. A spear screen still blunts the charge and keeps your line mobile and hitting hard; square is what you reach for when a unit is caught in the open with cavalry on a bad angle.

Orbis vs schiltron. The hollow square comes in two variants with different trade-offs, not one generic stance:

  • Orbis (O, or the control bar’s Square (Orbis) button) is the last-stand ring. Its charge brace and offence sit at the square’s baseline, but it holds its nerve better than any other stance — casualties erode its morale more slowly, so a surrounded unit fighting to the end is less likely to break outright. Reach for it when a unit is cut off and simply needs to survive as long as possible.
  • Schiltron (Shift + O, or the control bar’s Schiltron button) is the cavalry specialist. Its denser hedge of levelled spears braces a charge harder than the orbis does — cavalry riding into a schiltron comes off worse than riding into an orbis — at the cost of hitting even softer in return, and it carries no morale bonus. Reach for it when the priority is breaking the charge itself, not outlasting a siege.

Both variants share the same hollow-square footprint and the same mobility penalty; only the charge-bracing strength, offence, and morale resilience differ.

Enemy coordination

The enemy’s regiments don’t fight in isolation — a subcommander watches the whole group and hands each unit leader a standing directive on top of its own battlefield instincts. Three behaviours come out of this:

  • Line integrity — a unit that races far ahead of its neighbors gets held back near the group’s own depth instead of closing on the enemy alone.
  • Mutual support — an unengaged neighbor gets sent to guard an ally that’s already fighting, rather than standing idle nearby.
  • Flank coverage — a unit repositions to cover a group flank an enemy is bearing down on, before casualties or a maneuver leave it open.

A directive never overrides a unit’s own immediate reactions — turning to face a flank threat, forming anti-cavalry square, and calling for relief all still come first. It only shapes what an otherwise-idle unit does next, so the enemy line holds together without losing the individual judgement it already had.

The general

Above the subcommander sits a general, driven by a doctrine profile — data, not code, so a non-programmer can author a new one. Each profile sets:

  • Which army plans it knows — an advance-in-line or an envelop split into two wings, picked each AI tick against how the battle actually looks (an envelop needs a real numeric edge over the enemy, not just an even fight).
  • A reserve fraction — how many units start held back instead of joining the active line.
  • A reserve-commit threshold — once the active line’s own weighted-average morale drops below this, the reserves fold in.
  • Whether to pursue routers — an aggressive doctrine chases a broken enemy down; a cautious one holds the line instead.

Two doctrines reading the identical battle can commit to different plans. A held-back reserve doesn’t just sit inert, either — it still turns to face a flank threat, forms anti-cavalry square, or answers a nearby ally’s call for relief, exactly like any other unit; only its own advance is put on hold.

Weapon reach

Each weapon has an effective reach — how close two units must get before they trade blows. A longer weapon reaches that contact distance first, so it lands the opening strikes of a clash while a shorter-weapon enemy is still closing the gap:

  • Spears reach furthest (~2.4 m) — spearmen strike first as the lines meet, on top of blunting cavalry charges.
  • Swords (infantry, cavalry) are the mid-length baseline (~1.3–1.5 m).
  • Archers carry only a short sidearm (~0.6 m); they’re meant to shoot, not duel in the press.

So a spear line meeting a sword line head-on gets free hits during the approach — another reason to lead with spearmen and let the shorter weapons pin and flank. Combat resolves per soldier by weapon reach: a spearman strikes any enemy within its longer reach, while a swordsman beyond its own (shorter) reach cannot strike back.

That opening-strike edge now holds up once the lines are locked, not just on the approach. A spearman doesn’t back away once a swordsman closes in — it holds its ground and keeps fighting, and its reach advantage keeps landing hits the same way it did on the approach. Landed strikes already shove the struck soldier back (see “Knockback now pushes both ways” below), so a longer-reach line pushing a shorter-reach one back is a real consequence of winning the exchange, not a separate rule bolted on top of it. The swordsman, for its part, keeps pressing forward until it closes inside its own reach — standing just outside the spear’s edge gains it nothing, so it closes the gap rather than trading blows it can’t answer. A spear line facing a sword line therefore holds its position and keeps landing free hits while the swordsmen work to close the last stretch, instead of both sides settling to a common distance.

A regiment’s front line isn’t the only rank that can strike, either: how many ranks deep a unit’s engaged tier runs scales with its own weapon’s reach and how tightly its ranks are packed — a long-reach spear regiment can field several ranks able to strike an enemy within their own weapon’s reach, while a widely-spaced cavalry regiment (a mounted soldier takes far more ground nose to tail) only ever fields its front rank.

Knockback now pushes both ways. Every blow obeys Newton’s third law: the struck soldier is shoved back as before, but the striking soldier also recoils along the same line, split between the two by effective mass (bracing counts as extra mass). A light, unbraced attacker rocks backward hard on contact; a heavy or set formation barely moves. That’s why a cavalry charge into a braced spear wall now visibly backfires on the horses themselves, not just on the spearmen holding their ground — the heavier, set defenders absorb most of the shove and hand a proportionally bigger kick back to the lighter attacker.

Movement speed

Units move at realistic real-world speeds, measured in metres per second, with three independent paces per type — walk, jog, and sprint (see Pace modes below) — scaled to how much each type’s panoply weighs:

Heavier armor and weapons cost proportionally more at a run than at a walk — walking pace barely changes under load, but running form degrades fast, so spearmen’s walk sits close to the lighter types’ while their sprint lags well behind. Cavalry’s walk and jog map onto the horse’s own real walk/trot gaits rather than a rider-weight-scaled fraction, since a rider’s kit is a small fraction of what the horse itself carries. Its charge pace is deliberately toned down from a true historical gallop (which would close distance faster than the map/camera scale reads well) toward a fast canter — see docs/SPEED_VERIFICATION.md for the historical comparison.

That sprint gap is a lever: cavalry cross the field and reach an exposed flank far sooner than infantry can re-form to face them, so commit horse decisively and time your foot to be where the charge lands. (The faster the gallop at the moment of contact, the bigger the charge bonus — so a clean run-up hits harder than a standing start.)

Pace modes

By default, foot units advance on AUTO pace: they walk on the approach, jog when a ranged enemy is firing at them (to spend less time under missiles), and sprint at full speed once within about 200 px of the target — so a charge arrives at full momentum without wearing out the troops on the march.

Check Walk advance in the unit settings panel to lock the selection to walk pace throughout. This is required for formed stances like shield wall or pike phalanx: those formations hold their shape only at a walk, and an auto-sprint will break them. Keep Walk advance on whenever you are using a tight formation that would crumble if the unit broke into a run — spearmen start with it on by default; every other type starts with it off.

The AUTO sprint-override also matters for charge timing: a unit that enters sprint range will accelerate through any missile fire it still faces — gaining maximum speed at contact rather than jogging through the kill zone. Use that to your advantage when committing cavalry or an assault infantry charge.

Choosing a gait by click count

AUTO pace is the default, but you can lock a move order to a specific gait instead: right-click a destination once for Walk, twice for Jog, three times for Run, and four or more times for Sprint — click the same spot again within the multi-click window to add another click to the count. A click on a new destination (or one far enough from the last click, or one that arrives after the window has passed) restarts the count at Walk.

There are only three real paces per unit type (for cavalry, walk/trot/gallop — see the speed table above). Run has no pace of its own: it holds jog speed while still far from the destination and switches to sprint speed once inside the same close-range threshold AUTO uses, so it’s a distance-gated mix of the two rather than a fourth gait.

This overrides AUTO for that order: an explicit gait holds for the whole move, ignoring the fire/distance triggers AUTO would otherwise use. Reach for a chosen gait when you want a unit to hold a slow, orderly walk past a threat it would otherwise jog or sprint through (screening a flank, keeping a formation intact under fire without also toggling Walk advance), or to force an early sprint before AUTO’s close-range threshold would normally trigger it.

Side-stepping the line

A small lateral move order is carried out as a side-step: the unit shuffles sideways while holding its facing, instead of pivoting to face the move and pivoting back. That matters when you are dressing a line just before contact. Pivoting a regiment turns it away from the enemy for the length of the turn — its front momentarily points sideways, and the ranks lose their dressing while the soldiers reshuffle. A side-step keeps every soldier looking at the enemy and keeps the line aligned, so you can nudge a unit a few paces to close a gap or overlap an enemy flank without ever presenting your own flank in the process.

The cut-off is roughly a unit’s own width: a move that heads forward, wider than that or not, turns and marches normally. A wider lateral shift is a different maneuver again — see the lateral pivot below — rather than a plain centre pivot. So to reposition laterally in good order, make the adjustment small — a short step keeps the line intact, a long one commits the unit to a pivot and a march.

The lateral pivot

A move too far to the side to side-step, but not far enough off-facing to count as a move to the rear, is a file march: the regiment quarter-turns toward the destination’s side in place, marches straight there keeping its pre-turn footprint — the block never reforms into a line facing the new heading, so it stays exactly the shape it started as, moving sideways relative to its own printed grid — and then quarter-turns back to its original facing once it arrives. The unit ends up facing precisely the way it started; the reorientation only ever served the march itself.

Relative to the new heading, this reads as ranks and files trading places for the length of the march: what was the frontage (spread across the line) becomes the marching column’s depth, and what was the depth becomes its narrow frontage — soldiers advance in file, the way a column on the road does, not shoulder to shoulder. From the outside the block’s world silhouette doesn’t visibly rotate at all while it travels — only the individual soldiers’ own facing, and the direction they’re headed, change. Because nothing ever reforms, a lateral pivot has no countermarch cost the way a move to the rear’s reform does — a depleted unit executes it exactly as fast as a fresh one.

Backing up costs time

An in-place maneuver that makes men back up takes longer than one where they step forward. When a formation reshapes without the block marching — a frontage change, a centre pivot on the spot — some soldiers have to move backward relative to the way they face to reach their new slots. Troops shuffle backward at only a fraction of their forward (jog) pace, so those men lag, and the whole maneuver settles more slowly than it would if everyone were stepping forward. That fraction varies by type — a heavily-kitted Spearman shuffles backward at barely a third of his jog pace, while a lighter Archer is closer to half — so a reshape’s settling time depends on which type is doing the backing up, not just how far it must move. The practical upshot: a reshape that pushes the rear ranks backward (deepening the line) is the slowest to dress, and slowest of all for the heaviest-kitted troops. Order it with a margin of time before contact, not at the moment of impact.

The about-face (conversio) and quarter-turn are not reshapes, so this penalty doesn’t touch them: every soldier turns in place and holds their exact position for the whole turn — nobody steps forward or backward at all, only facing changes. Their duration depends solely on the drill turn rate, not the backward-speed cap.

The reform after an about-face is where the penalty comes back. Flipping a unit that has taken losses puts its short, under-strength rank — previously the safe rear rank — at the new front, so a rear move reforms after the turn: the men counter-march through the block onto ranks squared to the new heading, bringing a full rank forward and sending the short rank back. Some of those men are stepping backward relative to their facing, so a deep, depleted block takes a couple of seconds to dress. With Reform before move on, that time is spent before the unit steps off — order the retreat with a margin. With it off, the unit marches at once and reforms on arrival, buying speed at the price of leading the whole march with its thinnest rank.

This same file-countermarch mechanism is also available as its own standalone drill — see How to play: Controls for the Countermarch (exelismos) hotkeys. A rear-move order reforms incidentally, as a side effect of turning to march somewhere; the standalone drill runs it deliberately, always bringing a full rank forward, and offers a choice of where the whole regiment ends up afterward: the same ground (Choral/Persian), advanced (Macedonian), or withdrawn (Laconian). Use it instead of a rear-move order when you want the full-rank reform without also committing to a specific destination.

The centre pivot

A larger turn is a centre pivot: the unit rotates about its own centre onto its new heading in good order rather than snapping round. When the turn is sharp the unit pivots before it advances — it nearly halts, faces its destination, then marches — so it never slides sideways or backwards at speed. A destination to the rear therefore triggers a full 180° pivot: the whole regiment turns around to face where it is going before it steps off. (Don’t confuse that with the conversio about-face on V, where each soldier turns in place and the block does not rotate.)

The cost is time: a sharp reorientation spends a beat pivoting before the unit makes ground, and a 180° pivot spends the most. Plan turns ahead of contact — a unit told to reverse at the moment of impact will spend its pivot facing the wrong way. Note that a chasing or charging unit turns instantly instead, so an attack order never pays the pivot tax on the approach; the orderly centre pivot is reserved for your own move orders, where keeping the ranks dressed is worth the beat.

Re-facing to fight has a cost of its own. When a unit engages an enemy that is roughly a quarter-turn or more off its current front — attacked in the flank, or sent to hit a foe off to one side — it does not snap its facing round. Instead the whole line turns in place to bring its front to bear, the men holding their ground as the block pivots, and it withholds its blows until it has come about. A small correction still snaps and fights at once, so a unit already roughly fronting its target loses no time. The upshot: catching a unit on the flank buys you a genuine window — it must turn to answer you before it can strike back — so a flank attack rewards you both with the flank casualty bonus and the beat the enemy spends coming about. Once the turn settles, the unit by default just fights with whatever edge it now presents; the engine also supports reshaping the frontage right after the turn — back to the width it had before turning, or to match the frontage of the enemy it turned to face — reusing the same width-resize easing as a manual frontage drag, so the reshape never teleports the ranks. There’s no in-game control to pick a non-default reshape yet, so every unit still fights this out today.

The pivot itself also slows as the unit picks up speed — a unit turns fastest from a stand and tightens its turn rate the faster it’s already moving (down to 40% of the stationary rate at a full sprint), reflecting the real lateral force a moving body or formation can exert without losing footing or cohesion. So a turn ordered from a standstill snaps around quickest; one ordered mid-charge takes noticeably longer to come about.

Speed itself isn’t instant either: a unit ramps up to its target pace (walk, jog, or sprint) and ramps down when slowing or stopping, rather than snapping to a new speed. Heavier-kitted troops (spearmen) build up speed slower than lighter ones (archers), and every foot type slows down faster than it speeds up — stopping takes no effort, building momentum does. Cavalry is symmetric: a galloping horse can’t be reined in any faster than it can build up speed in the first place.

Arrival works the same way: a unit closing on its ordered point brakes over the final stretch and comes to rest on the marker, instead of charging to the wire and lurching to a halt past it. Only the final stop brakes — a queued waypoint route flows through its corners at pace, and a charge never slows: a unit closing on an enemy carries full speed into contact, so braking costs you nothing on the attack.

The same friction applies even when a unit loses its momentum without a deliberate stop — its last target dies with no new order queued, say. Its speed doesn’t just tick down to zero on the spot while the unit sits frozen: it keeps sliding forward along the direction it was actually heading, slowing under the same braking rate, until it drifts to a genuine halt. Watch a cavalry regiment that wipes out its target mid-charge: it carries real charge speed into that final moment, so it visibly coasts on for a beat before coming to rest, instead of stopping dead the instant it has nothing left to chase.

Wheeling on a flank

A wheel (Z left / C right) is the third way to change a line’s front, and the most useful in the line of battle. One end — the standing flank file — plants and holds while the rest of the block swings around it like a door on a hinge. The line ends up facing 90° across from where it started and lying along a new bearing, with every man still in his rank and file.

It sits between the two in-place turns and the centre pivot:

  • The quarter-turn (Q / E) changes facing but not place — the men turn where they stand and the block stays put.
  • The centre pivot (a move order) rotates the whole block about its middle, so both flanks sweep and the line stays centred on the same spot.
  • The wheel pins one flank and sweeps the other, so the line hinges rather than spinning in place — the near flank barely moves while the far flank marches a wide arc.

That hinge is what makes it a battle-line maneuver. Wheel a wing forward to take an enemy line in the flank — the ×1.5 side bonus (or ×2 in the rear) is the whole point of getting around them. Wheel a wing back to refuse a flank when the enemy laps around your end, presenting a fresh front instead of an exposed side. Because the far flank has to march an arc, a wheel takes time and covers ground: order it before contact, not in the grinding melee, and give the swinging flank room to travel. The swing is paced by the outer file — the corner man farthest from the hinge jogs his arc, and every inner file slows proportionally to hold the line straight — so a wide or deep block wheels noticeably slower than a compact one. A fighting unit ignores the order — you can’t hinge a line that’s locked in combat.

The same hinge swing also fires automatically as part of a large oblique move to the rear: if a rear-sector destination is oblique enough that the about-face’s own 180° reversal alone would still leave a sizeable turn to close, the regiment about-faces, THEN wheels the rest of the way onto the destination’s bearing, THEN marches — so a unit sent far to the rear on an angle steps off already lined up on its destination instead of correcting the last of the turn mid-march. You don’t order this one; it’s the drilled default for that shape of move (see how-to-play).

Cavalry never has to take that halt. Send a cavalry regiment on any sharply off-heading move — forward-oblique, to a flank, or to the rear, at any angle — and it wheels through the turn in one continuous swing while it keeps advancing, instead of stopping to about-face first. That makes a mounted wing meaningfully more responsive to redirect mid-battle: reroute it toward a fresh opening the instant one appears, without paying the halt-and-about-face cost a foot regiment given the same order still pays (see how-to-play). It’s also why an AI subcommander leading a cavalry wing can decompose its own directive into a wheel-then-march the moment the front shifts, rather than waiting for you to hand it a fresh order.

Order stances

Each order can carry a stance, armed with a hotkey before you click (a coloured cursor shows which is active; Esc clears it). Stances turn the same units into different tools:

  • Hold (H) — the unit stands its ground instead of chasing bait. Anchor a line, or keep a screen in place while your cavalry swings wide.
  • Attack flank (F) / Attack rear (R) — the unit maneuvers for the side or back rather than the front, cashing in the ×1.5 / ×2 multipliers above. Pair it with a frontal pin.
  • Skirmish (K) — a ranged unit kites: it backs off when a threat closes inside its firing range, trading ground to keep shooting rather than being caught in melee.
  • Support (G) — the unit guards a chosen ally, shadowing it and intercepting any enemy that closes on it. Good for gluing spearmen to vulnerable horse, or a guard onto your archers.
  • Cycle charge (J) — a caracole for cavalry: the unit charges, lands its impact hit, then peels back to a standoff and charges again, instead of grinding in a static melee where the charge bonus is already spent. The charge bonus scales with closing speed, so each fresh run lands hard — but don’t cycle into braced spears, where a fast charge backfires. The retire leg rides at a trot and brakes onto the standoff before wheeling about, and the run back in canters until the final sprint — so the loop keeps after a target that breaks and flees, instead of strolling off behind it and never re-engaging (Figure 19).
  • Sweep routers (,) — the unit prioritizes a routing (fleeing) enemy over a closer enemy that’s still standing and fighting, even as the gap to the router narrows. Arm it on a unit you want cleaning up broken formations rather than getting drawn into (or staying stuck in) a fresh melee with whoever happens to be nearest — a natural pairing with a flanking cavalry sweep once the enemy line starts to crack. The pursuit rides at a canter beyond the sprint window, so the sweeper actually gains on a fleeing router instead of strolling after it.
  • Roll the line (;) — once the current target routs or is destroyed, the unit automatically re-targets the next-closest enemy still fighting instead of standing idle or chasing a broken foe down. A single attack order can carry a unit through a whole line of weaker enemies one after another — useful once you’ve broken through a flank and want the advance to keep rolling without a fresh order for every foe.
  • Pin down (.) — a stall/pin trade for a unit confronted head-on: it swings roughly half as often as a normal stance, so it deals less damage over time — but each swing’s follow-through opens a brief window where its own defense is lowered, so a foe that catches it mid-swing hits harder than it otherwise would. Use it to hold a line cheaply in casualties from its own attacks, not to win a stand-up fight outright; it’s a genuine tradeoff, not a strict upgrade over a normal stance.
  • All-out attack (’) — a straight offense/defense trade: +20% effective attack, but the unit’s own defense drops 20% while it’s fighting all-out. It hits harder and dies faster at the same time, so use it to force a quick, costly decision — finishing off a wavering enemy, or a rearguard buying time — not as a strict upgrade over a normal stance.
  • Chase (\) — relentless pursuit: the unit overrides the normal disengage exit a plain move order (or losing steady contact) otherwise gives it, so it keeps closing on and re-engaging the same fleeing or repositioning enemy instead of breaking off. It also presses the approach at a canter beyond the sprint window (a plain attack conserves stamina at a walk out there), so “relentless” means it actually closes, not just that it never gives up. Use it to run down a routing foe rather than let it escape to rally and rejoin the fight later (Figure 20).
  • Wedge charge (-) — maximizes the approach at the cost of defense and stamina: a faster pace toward the target, a lower own defense while fighting, and faster fatigue buildup while engaged. Use it to punch a hole in the enemy line before it can brace, not as a strict upgrade over a normal stance — the unit that arrives first is also the easier one to hit.
  • Knockback focus (=) — an offense-only trade: the unit hits 40% softer, but every blow shoves the enemy back much harder and is more likely to fell it outright. Hold Shift when you arm or issue it to pick the push distance: the plain hotkey shoves a struck enemy just far enough to clear the battle line, while Shift+hotkey drives it much further back. Use it to break up a packed formation or buy room to maneuver, not to win a straight fight — the softer hits mean it trades its own damage output for control of space.
  • Give ground (`) — a deliberate withdrawal: the unit backs away from its nearest enemy at a steady pace while it keeps fighting, regardless of weapon reach on either side. This is the explicit, player-commanded counterpart to Weapon reach’s passive standoff above — that mechanic only ever holds a longer-reach unit’s ground or presses a shorter-reach one forward; it never backs a unit away on its own. Arm it when you want to trade space for time — peeling a spent line back toward reinforcements, or buying a moment for a flank to arrive — without breaking off the fight entirely.

Push stance (/) is the aggressive, offensive counterpart to Give ground: an engaged unit continuously applies physical pressure to drive its opponent backwards. Where Give ground yields territory while fighting, Push stance advances into the enemy line, pushing opposing soldiers back while maintaining melee contact.

Disengage and step back (Ctrl + ↓) is different in kind from the stances above: a one-shot maneuver, not a standing order. Where Give ground keeps fighting while backing away, disengaging breaks the fight off entirely — the unit stops striking back and steps a short, fixed distance to the rear, holding facing. Use it to pull a unit out of a fight that isn’t worth finishing, so it can regroup or be redirected elsewhere, rather than to hold a losing line (that’s what Give ground is for). It costs something too: the enemy’s own attack order doesn’t release just because you stopped fighting back, so it keeps swinging — with the flank/rear bonus — for as long as it can still reach the retreating line.

Disengage with sacrifice (Shift + Ctrl + ↓) is a harsher variant for heavily pressed regiments. A small rearguard detachment (~10% of remaining soldiers) stays engaged and is sacrificed as casualties to screen the main body’s retreat. The rearguard’s sacrifice delays the advancing enemy, slowing their pursuit while the surviving soldiers step back to safety.

A volley is not instant: the arrows fly a real arc and land where they were aimed a moment later, so a fast unit can partly outrun a shot loosed at long range, and casualties arrive on the beat the arrows come down rather than the instant they leave the bow.

Group attack distribution

When you have several units selected and right-click a target, press X to cycle between two group-order modes. The same toggle governs both an attack on an enemy and a relief order on a friendly (see Passage of lines) — which pair of behaviours applies depends only on what you right-click:

  • Focused (default) — every selected unit charges the same enemy, or (on a friendly target) every reliever swaps with the one tired unit clicked. Use it to overwhelm a single regiment quickly.
  • Distributed — each unit picks a different nearby enemy, sorted by proximity to the clicked target; on a friendly target, each reliever instead pairs with its own nearby engaged ally the same way. Extra units cycle through the list if you have more units than targets. Use it to hold an entire line rather than piling onto one regiment (or one relief swap) and leaving the rest unengaged.

The flash message in the top-centre of the screen confirms the active mode. The mode is sticky — it stays set until you press X again (Shift + X cycles the other way).

Terrain

Two types of terrain patch appear on the battle field:

Units automatically path around impassable terrain when ordered to move. A formed regiment takes the detour in good order: it steers by the corridor’s long-run direction and pivots onto it no faster than its corner man can run, so the block holds rank-and-file around the turn instead of smearing into a crowd. The forest is a different trade-off: going around it costs distance; going through it costs time. Generally it is faster to push cavalry or fast-moving infantry through rather than around a narrow forest.

The obstacle a unit routes around is the terrain you see, plus the unit’s own footprint: each block keeps a margin equal to its corner man’s reach from the centre, so a small squad slips through a lane that a wide battle line must round with a broad swing. A lane that looks clear of the hill is clear — for any unit narrow enough to fit through it. The walked detour hugs that margin the whole way around: straight legs run along the obstacle’s own grown boundary rather than a wider routing-grid lane, so two blocks of different widths round the same hill on two visibly different tracks (Figure 25).

A routing unit paths around a hill the same way while fleeing — it’s no longer a straight sprint toward the back edge. If a router is boxed in with every escape direction blocked, it gives up fleeing and stands to fight rather than running in place against the obstacle.

TipForests slow cavalry charges

A cavalry unit that contacts the enemy while still inside a forest hits with a reduced charge bonus — it can’t build full speed in the trees. Stage your charges from open ground whenever possible.

Order-response delay

Units don’t react instantly to orders. There is a brief reaction delay before they execute — about 0.5 s for infantry and spearmen, 0.3 s for cavalry. A unit already in melee is not slowed by the delay.

Drill commands (the wheel, about-face, quarter-turn, and countermarch) carry their own, shorter drill response beat — about 0.2 s by default between the command and the men starting the evolution. A drill is a rehearsed, single-evolution response to a direct order, so the beat is quicker than planning a fresh march. It is a per-unit characteristic: an elite regiment can be staged with a snappier beat and a raw levy with a sluggish one, so the same command visibly ripples through a mixed line.

This models the time for orders to travel down the chain of command. In practice:

  • Commit cavalry early. If you wait until the last moment to redirect horse, they may not reach the flank in time.
  • Feints are risky. A unit you pull away from melee takes rear hits during the disengage and takes a moment to start moving — don’t disengage into a second threat without enough margin.

Disengaging — a risky lever

Right-click the ground (a move order) to pull a unit out of melee. It’s how you retreat a battered regiment or redeploy cavalry to a fresh flank.

WarningDisengaging exposes your back

While a unit marches away it shows its rear to whatever it left — so that enemy gets free rear hits (×2) until your unit is clear. Pull out at the right moment, or pull out into a disaster.

Stop the unit and it re-engages anything nearby. Timing the disengage — late enough that the enemy is committed, early enough that you survive the exit — is one of the game’s sharper decisions.
